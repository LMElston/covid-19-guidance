"or" concept

Overview

Purpose
  • To clearly demonstrate there are options
  • To make the options, and number of options, easily scannable
  • To ensure a user can easily identify which block of content applies to them so they don't spend time reading unneccessary content, before they see the next option (which is the default experience when content is stacked vertically)
Looks like

Header A: Option 1 of 2

This is content for header A

Header B: Option 1 of 2

This is content for header B

(The visual design of the 'or' is to bluntly delineate the choices)

Why CSS injects the "or" text

Symmentically, it doesn't make sense to have an "or" structured under one heading trying to bridge two headings:

<h3>Header 1</h3>
<p>Content goes here.</p>
<p>or</p>
<h3>Header 2</h3>
<p>Content goes here.</p>

So we are proposing injecting the "or" with CSS and then adding invisible descriptive text to the end of the headings to support screen readers:

<h3>Header 1<span class="wb-inv">: Option 1 of 2</span></h3>
<p>Content goes here.</p>
<h3>Header 2<span class="wb-inv">: Option 2 of 2</span></h3>
<p>Content goes here.</p>

Hours per week you can claim

The hours you can claim are based on whether the space is a common (shared) area or exclusively dedicated as a work space.

Common (shared) area: Option 1 of 2

This space can only be claimed for the hours it is used for employment purposes. For example, if you work 40 hours a week at the dining table:

  •    40 hours (hours worked)
  • divided by 168 hours (total hours in a week)
  • times 100 (converting into a percentage)
  • equals 23.8% (percentage of time you can claim for the work space)
Designated room: Option 2 of 2

You can claim the full 24 hours per day, 7 days per week, for the use of that room when calculating the work space. For example, if you work 40 hours a week in your dedicated room:

  •    40 hours (hours worked)
  • equals 100% (percentage of time you can claim for the work space)

Example

Working in a designated room

Chris lives in a 7 room house and has a designated area in the home where Chris only performs employment duties.

  •  20 m2 (area of Chris' work space in his home)
  • divided by 200 m2 (total finished area)
  • times 100 (converting into a percentage)
  • equals 10% (percentage of home Chris uses for work space)

If Chris was sharing this work space with someone (Danielle) who was also using the room as a dedicated work space, this gives them two options:

  • One person claims
  • Both claim
One person claims: Option 1 of 2

Chris or Danielle could claim 10% of the work-space-in-the-home expenses such as water and electricity.

Both claim: Option 2 of 2

Chris and Danielle could also share the claim but would only be able to claim a total of 10% of the expenses between the two of them.

Working in a common (shared) area

Sam and Terry rent an 8 room house. Due to COVID-19, they have been working from home using their dining room table.

  •  10 m2 (area used for work space in the home)
  • divided by 120 m2 (total finished area)
  • times 100 (converting into a percentage)
  • equals 12% (percentage of home you use for work space)

If 40 hours a week are spent working at the dining table:

  • 12% (percentage of home used for work space)
  • times 23.8% (40 hours worked divided by 168 total hours in a week)
  • equals 2.8%

This gives them two options:

  • One person claims
  • Both claim

One person claims: Option 1 of 2

Sam or Terry could claim 2.8% of the work-space-in-the-home expenses such as water and electricity.

Both claim: Option 2 of 2

Sam and Terry could also share the claim but would only be able to claim a total of 2.8% of the expenses between the two of them.

"and" concept

Overview

Purpose

This design has limited use as heading don't typically bridge and connect to each other. When used, it is:

  • To clearly demonstrate there are content blocks that are complimentary/associated
Looks like

Header A-2

This is content for header A-2

Header B-2

This is content for header B-2

(The visual design of the 'and' is an open pipe to demonstrate that the content flows from one to the next)

Why CSS injects the "and" text

Symmentically, it doesn't make sense to have an "and" structured under one heading trying to bridge two headings:

<h3>Header 1</h3>
<p>Content goes here.</p>
<p>and</p>
<h3>Header 2</h3>
<p>Content goes here.</p>

So we are proposing injecting the "and" with CSS. (Unlike the "or" design, there is no need to add descriptive text to the end of the headings to support screen readers, as the content blocks are not mutually exclusive and can read independantly):

<h3>Header 1</h3>
<p>Content goes here.</p>
<h3>Header 2</h3>
<p>Content goes here.</p>
