How much of the work space can be claimed

Simplified option

To simplify the claim for your work space and office supply expenses, you can claim a flat rate of $x on your 2020 tax return.

To determine the amount of home expenses you can claim for your work space, you should separate the expenses between your employment use and non-employment use of your home. To calculate the reasonable percentage, you need to consider the size and the use of the room.

Size of the home and work space

Size of home

All finished areas within the home count towards the size of the home. This includes:

  • hallways
  • bathrooms
  • kitchen

You can measure in square metres or square feet.

Size of work space

The size of the space you work in. The claim must be reasonable.

If you have an open concept design, you would use a reasonable percentage of the open space to claim as your work space. You cannot use the entire floor space to represent your work space.

You can measure in square metres or square feet.

Determine the percentage of your home you use as a work space

To determine the percentage of your home you use as a work space is, use this calculation:

Size of work space divided by Size of home times 100 equals % of work space in the home

Example: Basic calculation
  •    40 m2 (size of work space)
  • divided by 400 m2 (size of home)
  • times 100 (to convert it into a percentage)
  • equals    10% (percentage of home used as a work space)
Example: Dining room table is the work space

Due to the COVID-19 pandemic, Sam has been working from home using her dining room table. The dining room is 12% of the total square footage of her house and she uses it for work for 40 hours out of a total 168 hours in the week.

Since the room is not used only for work, Sam will need to calculate the employment use of that work space.

  •    12% (size of work space compared to the entire finished area of the home)
  • times 23.8% (40 hours worked per week divided by 168 hours in a week)
  • times 100 (converting into a percentage)
  • equals     2.8% (percentage of home that is used as a work space)

If she paid $1,200 for electricity, heat, and water for the period she worked at home because of COVID-19, the employment use portion is $1,200 x 2.8% = $33.6

The calculator will perform this calculation for you.

Types of work spaces

There are two types of work spaces:

  • common (shared) area
  • designated room

Common (shared) area: Option 1 of 2

A common area is a space that has other purposes besides your work (for example, working at a kitchen table or using the family computer room). You can claim this space only for the hours you use it for your work.

Designated room: Option 2 of 2

A designated room is used only for your work (for example, a home office).

Hours per week you can claim

The hours you can claim are based on whether the space is a common (shared) area or designated room (dedicated exclusively as a work space).

Common (shared) area: Option 1 of 2

You can claim this space only for the hours it is used for work. For example, if you work 40 hours a week at the kitchen table:

  •    40 hours (hours worked)
  • divided by 168 hours (total hours in a week)
  • times 100 (to convert into a percentage)
  • equals 23.8% (percentage of time you can claim for the work space)

Designated room: Option 2 of 2

You can claim this room the full 24 hours per day, 7 days per week, for the use of that room when calculating the work space. For example, if you work 40 hours a week in your dedicated room:

  •    40 hours (hours worked)
  • equals 100% (percentage of time you can claim for the work space)

The calculator will perform this calculation for you.

Number of work spaces and workers in the home

If only one person has a work space

If only one person in your home uses a work space, that person will claim the expenses.

If multiple people are working in the same home but in different work spaces

If multiple people are working from your home, each in their own work space, they each calculate and claim the expenses separately.

If multiple people are sharing the same work space

If multiple people are sharing the same work space in your home, they have two options:

Share the claim: Option 1 of 2

The workers can divide and share the amount of the claim, but the total of their claims cannot be more than the full claim amount.

One person claims: Option 1 of 2

One person claims the full amount.

Examples

Working in a designated room

Chris lives in a 7-room house and has a designated area where only Chris performs employment duties.

  •  20 m2 (area of Chris' work space in his home)
  • divided by 200 m2 (total finished area)
  • times 100 (converting into a percentage)
  • equals 10% (percentage of home Chris uses for his work space)

If Chris was sharing this work space with someone (Danielle) who was also using the room as a dedicated work space, they have two options:

  • One person claims
  • Both claim

One person claims: Option 1 of 2

Chris or Danielle could claim 10% of the work space in the home expenses such as water and electricity.

Both claim: Option 2 of 2

Chris and Danielle can also share the claim but they would only be able to claim a total of 10% of the expenses between the two of them.

Working in a common (shared) area

Sam and Terry rent an 8-room house. Due to the COVID-19 pandemic, they have been working from home using their dining room table.

  •  10 m2 (area used for a work space in the home)
  • divided by 120 m2 (total finished area of the house)
  • times 100 (to convert into a percentage)
  • equals 12% (percentage of home used for their work space)

If they spent 40 hours a week working at the dining table:

  • 12% (percentage of home used for work space)
  • times 23.8% (40 hours worked divided by 168 total hours in a week)
  • times 100 (to convert into a percentage)
  • equals 2.8%

This gives them two options:

  • One person claims
  • Both claim

One person claims: Option 1 of 2

Sam or Terry could claim 2.8% of the work space expenses, such as water and electricity.

Both claim: Option 2 of 2

Sam and Terry can share the claim, but they would only be able to claim a total of 2.8% of the expenses between the two of them.
