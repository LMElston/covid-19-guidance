How, when, and where to get vaccinated

Provinces and territories are responsible for vaccinating their residents, including:

  • whether or not you need to register
  • which groups are getting vaccinated, in what order
  • when vaccinations will be available

Who is eligible for the vaccine

Vaccines will be available at no charge over the course of 2021 to everyone in Canada. This includes those who aren't citizens and applies to:

  • people over the age of:
    • 16 for the Pfizer-BioNTech vaccine
    • 18 for the Moderna vaccine
  • Canadian Armed Forces personnel that are on active duty abroad
  • diplomatic staff and their dependants

Public health measures continue to be essential in limiting infection while a large enough number of people across Canada are vaccinated.

When to consult a doctor

If you have questions or concerns about vaccination or if you are unsure if you should receive the COVID-19 vaccine, consult with your health care provider

You should consult your doctor or a health professional if you:

  • are immunocompromised due to a disease or treatment, such as chemotherapy
  • have an autoimmune condition
  • are pregnant or breastfeeding
  • have a bleeding disorder
  • have experienced mild to moderate immediate allergic reactions after a previous dose of authorized COVID-19 vaccines
  • have had a proven severe allergic reaction (for example, anaphylaxis) to any component of the specific COVID-19 vaccine or its container are experiencing any symptoms of COVID-19
  • are experiencing any symptoms of COVID-19

Recommended priority groups

The National Advisory Committee on Immunization has provided guidelines on who should get vaccinated first.

Your province or territory has the authority to decide who will be prioritized.

At-risk groups identified by the commitee

  • residents and staff of shared living settings who provide care for seniors
  • adults 70 years of age and older, with order of priority:
    1. beginning with adults 80 years of age and older
    2. decreasing the age limit by 5-year increments to age 70 as supply becomes available
  • frontline health care workers who have direct contact with patients, including:
    • personal support workers
    • those who work in health care settings
  • adults in Indigenous communities

As additional COVID-19 vaccines and supplies become available, the following populations should be offered vaccinations:

  • adults 50 to 69 years of age
  • first responders, including:
    • police
    • military
    • firefighters
    • coast guard
  • health care workers not included in the initial rollout
  • adults in racialized and marginalized communities
  • residents and staff of all other shared living settings, such as:
    • group homes
    • homeless shelters
    • correctional facilities
    • housing for migrant workers
  • essential primary caregivers for people who:
    • can’t care for themselves and
    • are at high risk of severe illness from COVID-19 due to advanced age (60 years of age or older)
  • essential workers who face additional risks to maintain services for the functioning of society, such as:
    • postal services
    • border services
    • rotational workers
    • grocery store staff
    • transportation workers
    • those involved in the pandemic response
    • food production or manufacturing workers
    • teachers and school or childcare staff not working virtually
How groups are chosen for early vaccination

The National Advisory Committee on Immunization (NACI) is an independent committee of experts that provides advice to the Public Health Agency of Canada. This advice is then distributed and published to inform decision making by provinces and territories, which are responsible for:

  • administering vaccines
  • deciding which populations will receive them first

Recommendations aim reduce serious illness and death while reducing disruptions in society.

The committee chooses groups for early vaccination by looking at risk factors for severe COVID-19 disease and outcomes, such as:

  • biological factors like:
    • advanced age
    • pre-existing medical conditions
  • social factors like:
    • low socioeconomic status
    • belonging to a racialized population

The decision-making process also includes a review of:

  • ethics
  • equity
  • feasibility
  • acceptability

These recommendations aim to reduce disruptions in society due to the pandemic by prioritizing those who:

  • provide essential services
  • take additional risks to protect the public

Recommendations are based on:

  • population-based analysis of the risks and benefits that considers:
    • risk of exposure to COVID-19, including:
      • ability to physically distance
      • access to other measures to prevent infection
    • risk of severe illness and death from COVID-19
    • how safe and effective authorized vaccines are in key populations
  • results of clinical trials
  • vaccine supply, which is the timing of available doses and the number of:
    • available vaccine types
    • doses each group needs
  • the current pandemic situation when vaccines become available

Provinces and territories may have to adjust their strategy depending on local trends and transmission rates.
